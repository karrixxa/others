## Honest reading (failures and regressions)

- **Competitive depression sharpens sustained dominance**: mean per-pattern
  dominance rises `0.627 -> 0.850`. On a held pattern the owner takes ~90–100% of
  cycles for three of four patterns instead of the baseline ~50% round-robin split.
  This is the intended effect — the loser update pushes rivals' participating
  weights down so they stop contesting the held pattern.
- **It regresses participation.** Distinct sustained winners drop `2.60 -> 2.00`
  and the dead-unit count rises `5.0 -> 5.6`. Depressing the losers of whichever
  neuron happens to win first lets an early winner capture multiple patterns and
  starves rivals — the same participation/dominance frontier documented in the
  memory notes.
- **Neither condition delivers clean one-to-one ownership.** Distinct winners stay
  at 2/4 (competitive) / ~2.6/4 (baseline), well short of 4 distinct owners, and
  fired-unit counts stay at 2–3/8. Consistent with the standing finding that real
  1-to-1 ownership is unsolved; this change removes the fictional learned reset
  magnitude and makes the depression local and charge-scaled, but is not a
  solution to allocation.
- Both conditions ran all seeds with no exceptions or shape/index drift; the reset
  invariant (`v_post == rest`, no post-reset recharge) held throughout (asserted in
  `test_competitive_reset.py::test_integration_zero_carryover_and_ei_trains`).

This report is diagnostic. It introduced no task-specific weights and no
pattern-aware exceptions; any center-pixel concentration emerges from the same
task-independent competitive-depression rule.
