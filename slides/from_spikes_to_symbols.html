02Why look beyond the LLM paradigm

The limits of globally trained intelligence

LLMs are extraordinary. But their power rests on structural commitments worth questioning.
  • Knowledge is learned by one global optimization over a large, mostly static dataset.
  • Updating it can mean retraining or bolt-on external memory.
  • Representations are powerful but distributed and hard to inspect.
  • The model is generally asked to emit another token even when its evidence is weak.

03A different foundation

Intelligence from local circuits

Not one model that sees everything at once — many small circuits that each learn from what is near them, and pass sparse symbols upward.
  • Local receptive fields and event-driven spikes
  • Local, continuous learning — no global error signal
  • Excitation and inhibition create stable specialists
  • Hierarchy composes symbols into concepts

05The first local circuit

The retina: center-ON, surround-OFF

A ganglion cell doesn't report raw light. It compares a small center against its surround — signalling local contrast, not a copy of every value.
  • Center excites; surround inhibits
  • Output encodes structure, not brightness
  • Lateral inhibition separates competing interpretations
From Spikes to Symbols05
06The minimal receptive field in this work

A 3×3 window learns oriented lines

A center-crossing 3×3 patch is the smallest neighborhood where a cell can learn relationships between a center and its surroundings.
  • Four primitives share the center pixel
  • Local cells become orientation-sensitive: horizontal, vertical, two diagonals
  • These features become inputs to the next layer
The beginning of a retinal-to-cortical hierarchy — built from repeated local windows.

07From competition to specialists

Competition creates specialist neurons

Many neurons compete to explain an input. A shared inhibitory neuron broadcasts a reset, so the best-matched unit wins repeatedly — and specializes.
  • Winner fires and strengthens its match
  • A shared inhibitory neuron resets the pool
  • Losing capacity is reallocated, recruiting new specialists
excitatory (E) inhibitory (I)

09The centerpiece

Hierarchies make symbols reusable

A higher layer never relearns raw pixels. It learns combinations of lower-level symbols.
  • Reuse: one primitive joins many larger concepts
  • Combinatorial generalization: known pieces form unseen combinations
  • Modularity: add concepts without redesigning the network
  • Interpretability: trace a concept back through its parts
From Spikes to Symbols09
10Scaling by repetition, not redesign

Size-agnostic learning through local features

A line is a line — in a 3×3 patch, inside a large image, at another position, as part of a letter or an object.
  • Scale by repeating local circuits across space
  • Each emits sparse symbols upward
  • Higher layers consume recognized features, not every raw pixel
Larger inputs need more receptive fields and routing — not a different learning rule.

11Composition as imagination

Composition becomes imagination

A stable symbol no longer needs the original sensory input to activate. Higher circuits can reactivate and recombine symbols into candidate situations.
  • Construct configurations never directly observed
  • Compare possible future states
  • Test alternative actions internally
  • Learn from prediction–observation mismatch

14Epistemic humility

Knowing, uncertainty, and the unknown

Knowledge should require a stable, strong internal representation — not merely producing an output.

Uncertainty emerges from observable dynamics: response strength, competitive margin, stability over time, and whether a reasoning chain completes.

"I recognize this."

One stable specialist with a strong competitive margin.

"I'm uncertain which."

Several nearly equal specialists — no clear winner.

"Known parts, new whole."

Recognized components in an unfamiliar arrangement.

"I have no representation."

No specialist reaches the required response — flag novelty, stop.

From Spikes to Symbols14
15Continuous learning

Continuous learning changes the compute equation

Learning and inference run through the same local circuit. A new event updates only the synapses it touches — the system keeps operating.
  • No network-wide backward pass, no global loss
  • No stored activation graph for backprop
  • Sparse updates tied to active events
  • No replay of the full historical dataset per update
From Spikes to Symbols15
16Data and bias

Less dependence on static datasets — and more visible bias

It doesn't remove the need for data; it changes data's role — from one giant frozen snapshot toward ongoing experience.
  • Immediate incorporation of new information
  • Adaptation to local, changing conditions
  • Fewer examples for concepts built from known parts
Bias isn't automatically solved — but it becomes measurable representational coverage instead of diffuse opacity.
From Spikes to Symbols16
17Scaling onto hardware

Scaling onto neuromorphic hardware

The theory maps naturally to spiking chips: event-driven, sparse, with memory and compute in the same place.
  • Local state · local synapses · spike routing
  • Learning directly on the device
  • Low activity when the world is unchanged
  • Unrelated regions run independently — no global gradient to wait on
Especially valuable for always-on robotics, edge devices, and adaptive sensors under limited power.

18Intellectual honesty

What is predicted vs. what is measured

architectural predictions

The composition, imagination, reasoning, and scaling described here are what the architecture is designed to enable. They follow from its principles — local receptive fields, owned symbols, local learning — and describe the long-term hypothesis this research pursues.

measured today

The working system is a small, from-scratch spiking network learning 3×3 line primitives with local rules and no gradients. Concrete speed, energy, and memory advantages on neuromorphic hardware are future validation — stated as expected sources of gain, not benchmarked numbers.

The gap between these two columns is the research program — not a claim already banked.

The thesis, in one sentence
Instead of training one enormous model to approximate everything it has seen, we can build intelligence from local systems that learn continuously, form explicit symbols, compose new possibilities, trace their own reasoning, and recognize the boundaries of their knowledge.
