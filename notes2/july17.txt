Lecture Transcript Summary (14:55–15:10)

This section focuses on why the current winner-take-all architecture is failing and explores possible solutions.

1. Pre-training may be needed before learning real patterns so the network starts in a stable state.

2. Learning compresses neuron weights, making neurons too similar. As variation disappears, multiple neurons fire together and winner-take-all fails.

3. Continuous-time simulations revealed that a clear winner exists initially, but after learning several neurons begin firing simultaneously.

4. Simply removing previous winners is not enough because the remaining neurons have also become too similar.

5. The current architecture uses one global inhibitory neuron. The instructor believes this is too simplistic because every winner shares the same inhibitory gate.

6. A possible redesign is one inhibitory neuron per excitatory neuron. This would provide localized inhibition, although gate saturation may still be a problem.

7. The cortical-column abstraction is probably too simple. Future models may require multiple neurons and outputs instead of one output neuron.

8. The retina already performs edge detection, suggesting higher layers may receive preprocessed edge information instead of learning it from scratch.

9. Another proposal is an always-active preprocessing layer followed by learning layers.

10. Pre-training creates a chicken-and-egg problem because every trained layer depends on another.

11. The instructor questions whether synaptic weights are the best abstraction and suggests moving toward membrane potential and ion-channel models.

12. Future models may include sodium flow, potassium pumps, membrane voltage, and more realistic biological dynamics.

13. Inhibition may dynamically increase a neuron's leak rate instead of acting as an instantaneous gate.

14. The refractory period may depend on biological recovery mechanisms rather than being a fixed time.

Key Takeaways:
- Pre-training may stabilize learning.
- Preserving variation between neurons is essential.
- Global inhibition is likely insufficient.
- More biologically realistic neuron models may solve current limitations.

1. THE MAIN GOAL
----------------

The goal is not merely to classify four patterns. The desired circuit must:

1. Receive one of four center-crossing 3x3 patterns.
2. Produce one stable L2E representative for each pattern.
3. Preserve unused neurons for future patterns.
4. Send feedback from the representative to the correct Layer-1 columns.
5. Use a local prediction/coincidence neuron and paired inhibitory neuron to
   suppress only the sensory activity explained by the prediction.
6. Leave unexplained activity as the prediction-error/free-energy proxy.
7. Eventually recognize a learned pattern rapidly, without requiring a long
   integration period at every level of a hierarchy.

The intended local Layer-1 column is:

    sensory/input E_i
           |
           +---- local sensory evidence ----+
                                           v
    L2E winner feedback -------------> prediction/coincidence E_i
                                           |
                                           v
                                  paired inhibitory I_i
                                           |
                                           v
                              inhibits the paired sensory/output E_i

This is the "two E plus one I" interpretation. The second E is not another
global classifier. It detects the local conjunction of present sensory
evidence and top-down feedback.


2. WHAT THE NEW LECTURE ADDS
----------------------------

A. Weight saturation can destroy competition even when the center problem
   is improved.

The update contains a saturating term such as:

    (1 - w / w_max)^2

This is useful for making a learned winner stable. However, if many competing
neurons are pushed into the same nonlinear/asymptotic region, their natural
differences become compressed. Their weights become more similar, they fire
at similar times, and winner-take-all becomes harder.

Important distinction:

- The true owner may enter the nonlinear stable region.
- Losing or unused neurons should not all be pushed there with it.

This adds a second causal hypothesis beyond the universal center pixel:

    common center dominance + population-wide weight compression

may jointly produce collisions, ties, and dead capacity.

B. A learned representation must eventually respond quickly.

The lecture argues that long integration delays would multiply across a deep
hierarchy. Therefore, final success is not only stable ownership. After a
pattern is learned, its representative should fire with much lower latency,
ideally from one appropriately timed volley rather than many repeated input
pulses.

Latency after learning must become an explicit outcome measure.

C. Prediction must be local coincidence, not a global feedback broadcast.

The lecture confirms why the earlier all-nine prediction behavior was wrong.
If feedback alone eventually drives every Layer-1 inhibitory column, every
learned pattern suppresses all nine inputs.

For column i, learning and prediction must depend on both:

1. The paired sensory/input E_i actually fired or locally depolarized the
   prediction neuron.
2. A real L2E representative sent feedback to that prediction neuron.

Only their local temporal coincidence should strengthen the decoder synapse
or make the prediction neuron fire.

D. Leak is necessary for the coincidence motif, not only for pattern-switch
   cleanup.

Suppose sensory evidence alone supplies half the prediction neuron's
threshold, or feedback alone supplies half. Without leak, repeated isolated
half-inputs accumulate. The neuron eventually fires even though the two
required inputs were never coincident. The intended AND-like motif becomes
an OR-over-time.

Therefore the prediction neuron needs biological membrane decay or an
equivalent ion-pump process satisfying:

- sensory alone does not eventually cause a prediction spike;
- feedback alone does not eventually cause a prediction spike during the
  immature/coincidence-learning state;
- appropriately timed sensory plus feedback can cause a spike;
- residual charge from an old pattern decays naturally;
- no artificial pattern-boundary clearing is used.

This makes the leak experiment more important than previously stated. It is
both a switching test and a temporal-coincidence correctness test.

E. The decoder has a genuine cold-start problem.

If decoder learning requires the prediction neuron to spike, but the decoder
weights are initially too weak to make it spike, learning stalls. This is
consistent with the earlier realistic decoder plateau.

The next candidate should test local subthreshold coincidence learning:

    delta d_ji = eta_d * z_j^R * u_i^S
                 * (1 - d_ji / d_max)^2

where:

- z_j^R is a short trace from a real L2E/R_j spike;
- u_i^S is local sensory-caused depolarization or eligibility at prediction
  neuron i;
- no label, pattern name, owner lock, or global reconstruction error is used;
- a prediction-neuron somatic spike is not required for early potentiation;
- actual inhibition still requires a physical prediction-neuron spike.

F. Pretraining is a hypothesis, not permission to use arbitrary balanced
   initialization.

The lecture suggests that a biological circuit may be partially configured
before pattern learning, so weights may begin in a moderate rather than tiny
range. But it also states that this process is uncertain and must respect
geometry and coupled dependencies.

Therefore:

- Do not simply assign convenient equal or random mid-range weights.
- Do not use balanced initialization as the ownership solution.
- If pretraining is tested, it needs its own biological mechanism and
  geometry-proportional consequences.
- Pretraining must not remove all free energy or eliminate plasticity before
  real patterns arrive.

G. The conceptual "half frequency" target is not enough to prove correct
   prediction.

The lecture uses suppression toward half-frequency as the conceptual point
where explained input contributes little or no free energy. But previous
experiments found that firing frequency alone did not distinguish correct
from incorrect prediction.

Therefore half-frequency may be an observed consequence, but it must not be
the sole correctness test or learning-stop gate. Correctness must also use
local pixel selectivity, causal timing, and residual activity.


3. WHAT THE EXPERIMENTS HAVE ACTUALLY TAUGHT US
-----------------------------------------------

Phase 27 - causal ownership audit

- In every persistent ownership collision studied, the eventual tyrant first
  developed a permanent advantage on the universal center input.
- The crossing event was split between self-spike potentiation and loser
  depression; neither mechanism alone explained every failure.
- Geometry/topology seed did not determine the outcome in that configuration.

Meaning: the center feature is a real causal amplifier, and simply removing
loser depression cannot guarantee correct ownership.

Phase 28A - common-input activity gate

- A continuous activity trace helped interleaved patterns.
- It failed on long holds because it could not distinguish "common across
  several patterns" from "active for a long time in one pattern."
- No tested condition passed the preregistered cross-schedule gate.

Meaning: do not implement or keep sweeping this exact gate. Its negative
result saved us from promoting a schedule-dependent mechanism.

Phase 29 - centered/covariance encoder

- The center/peripheral weight ratio fell by about 78 percent on the
  interleaved schedule.
- Same-step ambiguity decreased.
- Mean distinct ownership improved modestly.
- Persistent interleaved collisions did not disappear and became slightly
  more common by the binary collision metric.
- More neurons never fired.

Meaning: centered learning fixes the specific universal-feature overweighting
problem, but it does not solve recruitment or one-neuron-per-pattern
ownership by itself.

Paired versus global inhibition audit

- Paired first-responder collisions: 42/90 (0.4667).
- Global first-responder collisions: 62/90 (0.6889).
- Paired causal-winner collisions: 39/90 (0.4333).
- Global causal-winner collisions: 86/90 (0.9556).
- The causal-winner identities are not directly comparable because global
  uses one shared L2I while the paired experiment uses source-indexed L2I_j.

Meaning: paired inhibition substantially improves competitive breadth, but
it still does not prove stable pattern ownership. It is evidence for a useful
circuit direction, not a finished solution.


4. WHAT IS PROVEN, PARTIAL, AND STILL OPEN
------------------------------------------

Proven or strongly supported

- The universal center feature causally precedes multi-pattern capture.
- Loser depression can worsen the failure but is not its only cause.
- Centered learning strongly reduces center dominance.
- Per-column prediction and paired inhibition are more selective than a
  global all-nine broadcast.
- A plain continuous commonness trace is not robust across presentation
  schedules.
- Previous spike-only decoder training does not mature realistic decoder
  weights sufficiently.

Partial

- Paired inhibition reduces collision rates but does not create clean stable
  ownership in every run.
- Centered learning improves the intended weight pathology but can reduce
  recruitment.
- Local prediction selectivity works in held patterns, but switching
  carryover and realistic decoder maturation remain unresolved.

Still open

- Whether non-owner weights are being compressed into the nonlinear region.
- Whether a local subthreshold coincidence rule can solve decoder cold start.
- Whether leak creates a true temporal AND rather than delayed accumulation.
- Whether the active prediction loop improves later ownership or instead
  suppresses evidence needed for recruitment.
- Whether familiar-pattern latency becomes short enough after learning.
- How learning should stop locally without relying on firing frequency alone.
- Whether biologically justified pretraining is needed at all.


5. THE SMALLEST USEFUL NEXT EXPERIMENTS
---------------------------------------

Do not immediately run another enormous combined grid. Use sequential gates.

Gate 1 - saturation/compression audit

Measurement only. For winners, losing competitors, and never-fired neurons,
measure over time:

- fraction of weights in the linear versus nonlinear part of the saturation
  curve;
- variance and pairwise distance among competing weight vectors;
- first-response latency and tie rate;
- whether weight-vector compression precedes persistent collisions;
- whether loser depression or self-spike learning pushes non-owners into the
  compressed region.

This directly tests the new lecture hypothesis. Do not alter equations.

Gate 2 - decoder cold-start test

Compare the existing spike-gated decoder with the local subthreshold
coincidence rule. Require:

- no learning without an L2E feedback event;
- no learning without paired local sensory eligibility;
- bounded potentiation when both occur;
- inactive columns unchanged;
- realistic weights reach functional prediction without manual maturation.

If this fails, stop before the full-loop grid.

Gate 3 - temporal AND/leak test

Use one preregistered leak constant, not a parameter search. Test:

- repeated sensory-only inputs;
- repeated feedback-only inputs;
- correctly timed sensory plus feedback;
- mistimed sensory and feedback;
- continuous switches between patterns.

Success requires only the coincident condition to produce the intended
prediction response during the immature state, with old charge decaying
naturally.

Gate 4 - active local suppression test

Compare prediction shadow versus active prediction-to-paired-I_i. Measure:

- real L2E spike time;
- feedback delivery;
- prediction-neuron voltage and spike;
- paired I_i voltage and spike;
- inhibition delivery to the paired sensory/output E_i;
- residual activity before and after suppression;
- incorrect or inactive-column suppression.

Only after Gates 1-4 give interpretable results should the full 30-seed,
multi-condition integration grid run.


6. FINAL SUCCESS CRITERIA
-------------------------

The model is not finished until it demonstrates all of the following in the
same causal circuit:

- Four patterns produce four stable first-responder L2E owners.
- Spare L2E neurons remain available.
- The owner is established through physical temporal competition, not argmax.
- The learned owner eventually responds with short latency.
- Feedback reaches only the correct local prediction columns through learned
  weights.
- Prediction depends on local sensory-feedback coincidence while learning.
- Repeated single-source half-inputs do not accumulate into false prediction.
- A physical prediction spike drives the paired I_i.
- Only explained active sensory columns are suppressed.
- Novel or changed features remain active as prediction error.
- Pattern switching does not retain false prediction from the old pattern.
- Learning remains local and bounded.
- No labels, pattern names, owner locks, normalization, backpropagation,
  global reconstruction loss, hard-coded center exception, or arbitrary
  balanced initialization are used.

BOTTOM LINE
-----------

The new lecture does not replace the experiments already completed. It
clarifies why they are incomplete.

We have attacked the universal-center problem and learned that centered
learning helps but does not finish ownership. The new material says the next
missing mechanisms are likely:

1. preventing non-owner weight compression near saturation;
2. learning the decoder from local subthreshold sensory-feedback
   coincidence;
3. using leak to preserve true temporal coincidence and remove stale charge;
4. verifying selective paired inhibition and short post-learning latency.

These are the important next questions. More large parameter sweeps should
wait until these smaller causal gates pass.
